## THOUGHT PROCESS (the narrative — why this move, what we expect, what we're watching)
The binding *additive* constraint had moved exactly where SenseRun #8's seed predicted, and re-SENSE confirmed
it on disk rather than from memory: with CLD's structural path now executable (#8), the dominant gap was again
**coverage that also opens a new scoring path**, and the format that did both was **DYN**. The §8.1 self-audit
put Coverage at 3 (DYN/ARC/TRAP/BRIEF empty); Discrimination was lower at 2 but is unreachable inside a SenseRun
(it needs live-model response data the rail forbids), so the dominant *feasible* lever was Coverage, and among
the empty formats DYN was the dominant pick for a structural reason: it is the *only* empty format whose
deterministic oracle is already **governed** (Structure §3.1 "reference trajectory match"), so seeding it does
not merely add items — it opens a **third executable judge-independent path** and the **first auto-graded
surface for Dimension D** (weight 0.18), which until now was asserted in the rubric but exercised by no live
executable item. ARC/TRAP/BRIEF would have added items without a comparably clean new executable path (BRIEF is
pure jury → blocked behind the uncalibrated gate), so they were backlogged.

I treated this as *implementation, not rule-making*, the same discipline as #8. §3.1 and §4.6 already mandate
that DYN be graded by "reference trajectory match"; authoring the items and the scorer realizes a governed
method and invents no new rule, touches no A–E weight, and leaves the parked §3.1(b) accept-rule hardening (the
genuine Meadows #5) firmly with August + Ember. The one place I introduced numbers the prose didn't fix — the
component weights (0.40/0.25/0.15/0.10/0.10) and the trap cap (0.25) — I treated as tool-internal policy,
documented as tunable and flagged for calibration against real responses, exactly as the CLD scorer's
constants were.

The design choice that took the most care was what counts as falling into the **trap**. My first cut fired the
0.25 trap cap on the naive *mode* alone (predicting "goal-seeking"). But a smoke test exposed that this
conflated two genuinely different answers: a model that says "smooth approach to a *higher* plateau" for the
climate item got the *endpoint* right and only missed that the rise keeps going — that is a half-right answer,
not the naive picture — whereas the model that says "stabilizes at today's level" is the canonical stock-flow
misperception the item is built to catch. I tightened the trap to fire only on the **full trap trajectory**
(the naive mode *and* its endpoint), routing right-endpoint-wrong-mode to the 0.5 partial tier. This strictly
improved discrimination (the whole point of the format) and is the more faithful reading of "the intuitive
wrong trajectory." I held the fork open in the code comment and the item prose so a future calibration can
revisit it against real score distributions. I built the scorer in the house idiom — subcommands plus a
fail-closed `calibrate` self-test that reproduces known-true numbers (the oracle's own mode↔feature
consistency, by code) before any output is trusted — so the grader can re-derive its own validation set; it
does, 34/34.

What we expect: DYN's deterministic trajectory sub-score is now machine-executable end-to-end — a true third
judge-independent path alongside SF and CLD, and the first for Dimension D. What we are watching: (1) whether
real model outputs parse cleanly into the `{behavior_mode, overshoot, oscillation, delay_dominant,
eventual_direction}` schema, or whether (as flagged for CLD) a prompt-template / response-parsing layer is
needed to elicit it — the scorer normalizes case + a broad synonym set for modes and directions, but free-form
prose ("it'll spike then crash") may need a mapping step; (2) whether the trap cap and the insight gate
